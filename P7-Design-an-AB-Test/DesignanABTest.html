Experiment Design

Experiment Overview: Free Trial Screener

In the experiment, Udacity tested a change where if the student clicked "start free trial", they were asked how much time they had available to devote to the course. If the student indicated 5 or more hours per week, they would be taken through the checkout process as usual. If they indicated fewer than 5 hours per week, a message would appear indicating that Udacity courses usually require a greater time commitment for successful completion, and suggesting that the student might like to access the course materials for free. At this point, the student would have the option to continue enrolling in the free trial, or access the course materials for free instead.

The unit of diversion is a cookie, although if the student enrolls in the free trial, they are tracked by user-id from that point forward. The same user-id cannot enroll in the free trial twice. For users that do not enroll, their user-id is not tracked in the experiment, even if they were signed in when they visited the course overview page.

Metric Choice

Invariant Metrics

  • Number of cookies (number of unique cookies to view the course overview page): As the unit of diversion, an even distribution of cookies across the control and experiment group is expected. Furthermore, since a visit happens before the experiment takes place, the number of cookies visiting the page should not be affected.
  • Number of clicks (number of unique cookies to click the “Start free trial” button): Since the clicks happen before the free trial screener experiment is run, the number of clicks should not be affected by the experiment.
  • Click-through-probability (number of unique cookies to click the “Start free trial” button divided by number of unique cookies to view the course overview page): Similarly, since the clicks happen before the experiment is run, the click through probability should not be affected by the experiment.

Evaluation Metrics

  • Gross conversion (number of user-ids to complete checkout and enroll in the free trial divided by number of unique cookies to click the “Start free trial” button): This metric will be useful in determining the effect the experiment has on enrollment. Since gross conversion happens after the experiment, we will be able to see if the experiment causes a significantly significant change in gross conversion rates. (dmin=0.01)
  • Net conversion (number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14-day boundary divided by the number of unique cookies to click the “start free trial” button): This metric will be useful in determining the effect the experiment has on final profitability. Since net conversion happens after the experiment, we will be able to see if the experiment causes a significantly significant change in net conversion rates. (dmin=0.0075)

Unused Metrics

  • Number of user-ids (number of users who enroll in the free trial): Since user-ids are only tracted after a student signs up for the free trial, we would expect a different amount in our control and experiment groups. As such, it would not make a useful invariant metric. Since the number of user-ids is not standardized, it could fluctuate wildly between days and cloud our results. As such, it would not make a useful evaluation metric.
  • Retention (number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14-day boundary divided by the number of users-ids to complete checkout): At first glance, this metric was thought to be useful in determining the effect the experiment has on the number of students who leave the trial. Since retention happens after the experiment, we would be able to see if the experiment causes a significantly significant change in retention rates. Later in the analysis, the duration of an experiment with sufficient power to use retention was found to not be manageable.

Launching Decision

The ultimate goal of the experiment is to reduce the number of frustrated students who leave the free trial because they didn’t have enough time, while not significantly reducing the number of students to continue past the free trial and eventually complete the course. In order to reduce the amount of non-serious students signing up and using scarce coaching resources, a decrease in gross conversion rates will be required to launch. In order to make sure we don’t reduce the number of students who continue past the free trial, no reduction in net conversion rates will also be required to launch. In other words, I will be looking for less students to sign up for a free trial, but for no less students who do sign up sticking it out.

Measuring Standard Deviation

A bernoulli distribution is assumed with a standard deviation of sqrt(p*(1-p)/N). By comparing the unit of analysis and unit of diversion for each metric, we can evaluate whether the analytical estimate of standard deviation is accurate or if an empirical estimate would be needed.

  • Gross conversion Stdev = 0.020231
  • Retention Stdev = 0.054949
  • Net conversion Stdev = 0.015602

Since gross conversion and net conversion share the same unit of diversion as the unit of analysis (cookies), the analytical estimate of standard deviation should be accurate. Since retention does not share the same unit of diversion as the unit of analysis (user-ids vs cookies), the analytical estimate of the standard deviation may not be accurate. If I was using retention as a final evaluation metric, I would want to calculate the standard deviation empirically.

Sizing

Number of samples vs. Power

Since my evaluation metrics of gross conversion and net conversion are expected to be highly correlated, I will not use the Bonferroni correction as it would be too conservative.

This online calculator was used to determine the optimal sample size for the experiment based on an alpha of 0.05 and a beta of 0.2. The optimal sample size was divided by the rate that pageviewers click on the “Start free trial” button. This number was doubled to account for the control and experiment groups.

  • Pageviews = 685,325

Duration vs. Exposure

If 100% of Udacity’s traffic is diverted to the experiment, it will take about 18 days to complete. If 50% is diverted, it will take about 36 days. Both are within the client's expectations of a few weeks.

An experiment is generally considered high risk if it could harm someone or is collecting sensitive information. Since our experiment does neither and the chance of an adverse unintended financial consequence is low, I do not believe the experiment to be risky. Given this belief, I would recommend diverting 100% of Udacity’s traffic to the experiment.

Summary

The Bonferroni correction was not used in this analysis as the metrics were thought to be highly correlated and any use would have resulted in undue conservatism. Also, since our decision rule required that both gross conversion and net conversion had a significant result, the Bonferroni correction isn’t needed.

However, if more evaluation metrics were used, the chance of finding a significant result by chance would increase. In this case, it would be necessary to use the Bonferroni correction. Also, If we decide to do a post-test segmentation on the results, the Bonferroni correction might be needed.

Since there were no discrepancies between the effect size tests and the sign tests, I feel confident that our experiment had a significant negative effect on gross conversion and no significant effect on net conversion.

Recommendation

Our results showed the decrease in enrollments that we wanted, but it did not show the increase in retention that we were looking for. Financially speaking, it is possible that the reduced enrollments could even lead to less revenue without a subsequent increase in retention. Since our launching decision required both a reduced gross conversion and an increased net conversion, I would not recommend launching the change.

Follow-Up Experiment

We saw that filtering by time available didn’t lead to a significant decrease in the amount of early cancellations. Perhaps we can take a page from Robert Caildini’s book Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion and make our product appear more valuable to students. In the book, Caildini argues that when something appears scarce or exclusive, people assign more value to it.

I suggest running an experiment where we make the course appear more exclusive. When a student clicks “start free trial”, an experiment group will be given a prompt that says, “Thank you for your interest in our free trial. Because of high demand and limited coaching resources, we ask that you complete a quick proficiency test before proceeding.” The proficiency test will have 5 easy multiple choice questions that relate to the subject. It will be designed to be easily passed. If a student answers every question correct, they will be given a prompt that says, “You clearly deserve a spot in this course. Happy learning!”. If a student answers any question wrong, they will be a given a prompt that says, “Don’t be discouraged, perhaps you would prefer access to the free course materials”. At this point, the student would have the option of attempting the proficiency test again or to proceeding to the free course materials.

Null hypothesis: Students prompted to take the proficiency test will continue past the 14 day trial at the same rate as the control group.

Alternative hypothesis: Students prompted to take the proficiency test will continue past the 14 day trial at increased rates compared to the control group.

Unit of diversion: User_ids will be used to track participants in the control and experiment groups. We are only interested in users who sign up for a free trial, so user_ids will be sufficient. If we were interested in students who did not sign up, cookies would need to be used.

Invariant metric: Number of user-ids should be evenly distributed between the experiment and control groups. As such, user-ids will be used as an invariant metric.

Evaluation metric: Retention will be used to evaluate the experiment. Retention is the number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14-day boundary divided by the number of users-ids to complete checkout. Increased retention should correspond to reduced frustration among students.

Launch Decision: If there is a statistically and practically significant increase in retention, I will suggest launching the change.
